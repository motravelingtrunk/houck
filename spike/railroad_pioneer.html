Louis Houck: The Railroad Pioneer

Louis Houck practiced law through the 1880s, then decided to try a new business venture: railroading. Houck had no prior experience in railroading, but saw firsthand the increasing need for a railroad in Cape Girardeau, which would prove to be a successful business. After abandoning his law practice in the 1880s, Houck became involved with the Cape Girardeau Railway Company. Over the next 10 years he expanded his railroad line.

In July of 1891, Houck began construction on the St. Louis, Kennett & Southern Railroad, which took about 3 years to build. Houck first took control of an existing section of the Cotton Belt Rail Line than ran from Campbell, Missouri to Kennett, Missouri. Within 3 years, this existing line was added to, connecting Kennett to Caruthersville, Missouri. In addition, this railroad was able to reach the Mississippi river and connected with the Cotton Belt Rail Line at Campbell, Missouri. This connected with the St. Louis and Iron Mountain Railroad in Delta, Missouri, establishing an avenue of commerce between Southeast Missouri and St. Louis. Not only that, but once the Campbell line connected with the Cotton Belt Railroad, there was access by railroad from Delta, Missouri in the north all the way south to Arkansas.

The connection of Southeast Missouri to St. Louis by railroad opened up the market for merchants in the rural Southeast of Missouri. Rural communities were able to ship raw materials like timber and agricultural products to other regions, and St. Louis was able to ship its manufactured goods into these regions, opening up a market for them as well.

Houck constructed a second railroad line as well: the St. Louis, Cape Girardeau, Ft. Smith Railroad. This line extended to Southwest Missouri from Cape Girardeau to Hunter, Missouri. It provided Southwest communities the opportunity to ship their goods directly to the Mississippi River, and provided a more direct line to the Mississippi than Houck’s competitors (such as the St. Louis and Iron Mountain Railroad). The isolated regions of Southwest Missouri were now able to gain access to the railroad traffic, which they were isolated from before the creation of this railroad line.
