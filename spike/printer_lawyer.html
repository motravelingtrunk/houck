Louis Houck: The German Printer and Lawyer

Louis Houck was born in Mascoutah, Illinois in 1840 to German immigrant and printer Bartholomeaus Hauck. Bartholomeaus eventually made his way to Belleville, Illinois with his family where he opened the Belleviller Zeitung, which published its first issue on January 11, 1849. At just 9 years old, Louis Houck began working side-by-side with his father in the family printshop, learning the tools of the trade about publishing a German-language newspaper.

Growing up, Louis Houck’s father put him in the German Evangelical school in Belleville, Illinois, a Catholic school. Occasionally, Houck’s father sent him to Herman, Missouri where he received instruction in German from German newspaperman Emil Muehl. From his experience working with Muehl, Houck gained an interest in the stories German newspapers published, and had a deeper interest and respect for the German newspaper business.

Speaking little English, Houck went to the University of Wisconsin at age 17. He attended the university for 3 months and studied English, German, Latin, and Greek. After Houck returned to Illinois after his studies, he was asked by Politian William M. Morrison, Democrat, to start a German newspaper of his own in the interest of the Democratic Party. Houck agreed, and in 1859 he started Volksblatt.

When he was away at the University of Wisconsin, Houck became interested in the ideas and ideals of the Democratic Party. Most Germans in Illinois were Republicans and did not agree with what the Democrats stood for. So, Houck’s newspaper was not liked by people, especially Germans, in Belleville. Houck often wrote about how he did not agree with President Abraham Lincoln’s politics, which angered people—most people agreed with Lincoln. On May 10, 1864, a group of armed soldiers came and destroyed Volksblatt. Houck vowed that he would repair the damage and keep printing, although business efforts were eventually abandoned.

Louis Houck moved on from German printing and decided to travel down the law path. He was admitted to the Illinois Bar, which means he was licensed to practice law in the state of Illinois, and established a law practice in Cairo, Illinois around 1865. In 1869 Houck moved from Cairo to St. Louis, Missouri where he established a different law firm in partnership with Robert G. Ranney. That same year Houck was appointed Assistant United States District Attorney, a position he resigned from when Ulysses S. Grant became president in 1869.

Later in 1869 after coming to a dead end in his career, Houck decided to move to Cape Girardeau, Missouri stating that "a move from the city to the country would enable an individual to wield greater influence and accomplish more."
