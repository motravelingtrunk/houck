Immigration: Labor for the Railroad

During the construction of Houck’s railroads, there was not enough local manpower to help build the railroad and Houck was forced to hire laborers from outside the region. The construction of the St. Louis, Kennett & Southern Railroad especially needed more manpower than what was available.

Through the years 1893 and 1894 more than 200 workers, most of who were Irish immigrants, cleared, graded (leveling out the ground), and drove piles at various points in the survey line (the ground where the railroad was going to be constructed). These immigrants lived in camps on the site and worked on the railroad in separate groups. They would chop down tall grass, cut down hundreds of Cypress and Oak trees that were on the land, lead teams of Ox to remove tree stumps from the ground, and stacked lumber along the line route so the construction groups could quickly and easily access the material. During the construction of one of his later railroad lines, some of Houck’s local workers refused to work in the cold, leaving him no other choice but to bring in hundreds of new laborers from St. Louis. These laborers were both Greek and Italian, and interpreters came to Southeast Missouri with them as well so people could translate to English what they were saying. Upon hesitation, Houck also employed local African American workers as well.

Houck wanted workers that were smart and quick learning to be on his labor team, though with shortage of labor, that was not always the case. He had little patience for slow-learning workers, and became angry when he had to explain every step of the labor process to them—time is money, and every time Houck had to stop and explain something to a group of workers, he was losing profit.

Most of Houck’s railroads were poorly constructed. Even though Houck cut costs where he could in order to construct the railroads at a cheaper cost, the bad construction was largely due to the fact that most of Houck’s workers were unskilled in railroad construction—they did not have previous training in building railroads, they just needed a job in order to be able to make money to live off of.

Though Houck paid his workers, he often paid them late and not as frequently as he should have. He also regularly stopped construction and laid off the workers who were working on the project. In order for Houck to save money in his railroading venture and cut costs, these layoffs were necessary. Laid off workers often wrote letters to Houck explaining how being fired from the job affected their family and ability to have decent living conditions or food supply.

Not only did these layoffs affect workers, but they also effected the local merchants. Local merchants depended on these workers as consumers, or people who buy from the merchants. Without a salary or income, any money these laid off workers had went into buying food instead of buying goods from local merchants.

During the 1890s in the United States, laborers from across the country were going on strike for better salary, working conditions, and better and safer working hours. These labor strikes were especially hurtful to the railroading business with the outbreak of the Pullman Strike in the Summer of 1894, where people boycotted (stopped using) and disrupted railroad traffic in the Midwest in order to push for better labor benefits. Surprisingly, Houck’s workers did not go on strike. These workers believed that once the railroad was complete their economic condition would be improved. They also felt powerless against one of the most prominent men in Southeast Missouri, deciding that going on strike would do no good.
