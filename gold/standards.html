Gold vs Silver: The Gold Standard vs The Silver Standard

During the 1890s, one of the biggest political feuds revolved around which monetary coinage was to be used in the United States: Gold or Silver. This debate set the tone of the nation, sparking heated debates between both the Democrats and the Republicans. This issue was settled when the Republican nominee, William McKinley, defeated the Democratic nominee, William Jennings Bryan, in the 1896 presidential election. The Gold Standard was implemented.

The Gold Standard, first adopted in 1873, was favored by the Republican Party. This standard restricted the money supply that was in circulation, but was argued to have stable value. The stable value was thought to have been good for business, which in turn was thought to have been good for workers. These “Gold Bugs,” a nickname for supporters of the Gold Standard, believed that silver coinage would lead to fluctuation of values, ruin credit, and turn business decisions into a wild gamble.

The Silver Standard, or Free-Silver movement, was favored by the Democratic Party. This movement argued that a restricted money supply from gold would lead to lower wages and depressed prices for farmers and others. These “Silverites,” the nickname for supporters of the Free-Silver movement, pushed to add silver coins to the money supply, arguing that it would make more money available for higher wages, for farmers’ profits, and for people in debt who needed to pay off loans. They thought it would also boost profits for Western silver mine owners and benefit their employees.

Although this national debate had little effect on the Southeast Missouri region, it most definitely had an impact on Louis Houck and how he conducted his railroad business. Houck was a leading Free-Silver Democrat in the state of Missouri, pushing and promoting its benefits to those around him whenever he could, as well as paying his workers in silver coins. While constructing his railroads, and before any business decisions were made, he had to determine what effect a gold or silver standard would have on money that was invested in his project.

Houck felt that the national money supply was unstable, noting that the national economy was not as strong as it could have been. Because of this, Houck limited the amount of money he was willing to invest into the railroads. His hesitation on investment often led to abandoned projects, laying off workers, a delay in paying his workers, and an increased motivation to gather the interest of outside investors. The gold versus silver debate impacted how Houck decided to expand his railroad, which in turn had an impact on the economy of Southeast Missouri.
